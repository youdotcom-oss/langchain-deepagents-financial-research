# EU-27 GDP in 2025: Anomaly, Industry, and Macroeconomic Analysis

**Report Date:** May 8, 2026
**Coverage:** Calendar year 2025 (full-year), all 27 EU member states
**Primary sources:** Eurostat, European Commission Spring/Autumn forecasts, IMF Article IV, national statistical offices (CSO Ireland, INSEE, KSH, STATEC, Statistics Estonia, Bundesbank/Destatis)

---

## 1. Executive Summary

The EU-27 economy expanded **1.5%** in real terms in 2025 [[2]]. Beneath that aggregate, growth was unusually polarized:

- **Ireland was the dominant statistical outlier**, with headline real GDP up **12.3%** — driven almost entirely by multinational pharma/chemicals output, contract manufacturing, and IP-related effects, not by the underlying domestic economy [[1]][[2]][[3]][[4]].
- A second tier of **strong outperformers** (3–4% growth) clustered in small open service economies (Malta 4.0%, Cyprus 3.8%, Croatia 3.4%) and CEE convergence economies (Poland 3.6%, Bulgaria 3.1%) [[2]].
- **Denmark (2.9%)** was a unique developed-economy outperformer, driven by Novo Nordisk-led pharmaceutical exports [[2]][[5]].
- The **major laggards** were the EU's two large industrial economies, **Germany (0.2%)** and **Italy (0.5%)**, plus **Finland (0.2%)**, **Hungary (0.5%)**, **Austria (0.6%)**, **Estonia (0.6%)**, and **Luxembourg (0.6%)** [[2]]. The common thread: exposure to manufacturing/auto exports, US tariffs, and high-rate-lag drag on construction.
- **France (0.8%)** and **Romania (0.7%)** were below trend due to fiscal consolidation and political uncertainty, while **Slovakia (0.8%)** suffered the most severe US-tariff shock among small EU economies via its auto sector [[6]][[7]][[8]].

The unweighted simple-average growth rate across the 27 members is approximately **2.1%**, but the EU-weighted aggregate is dragged to 1.5% by the underperformance of Germany, France, and Italy — collectively over half of EU GDP in nominal terms [[1]].

## 4. Anomaly Analysis

Defining anomaly as growth ≥ EU mean + 1 pp (high) or ≤ EU mean − 0.7 pp (low):

**High-growth anomalies (≥ +2.5%):** Ireland, Malta, Cyprus, Poland, Croatia, Bulgaria, Denmark, Lithuania, Spain.

**Low-growth anomalies (≤ +0.8%):** Germany, Finland, Italy, Hungary, Austria, Estonia, Luxembourg, France, Romania, Slovakia.

**Statistical outlier:** Ireland's 12.3% is more than **8 standard deviations** from the EU mean and is structurally a national-accounts artefact of multinational activity — it should be flagged separately rather than treated as a normal cyclical reading [[3]][[4]].

## 5. Industry Breakdown for Anomalous Countries

### 5.1 High-growth anomalies

#### Ireland (+12.3%) — multinational distortion, not a normal expansion

Ireland's sectoral picture is dominated by foreign-owned multinationals concentrated in pharmaceuticals/chemicals, contract manufacturing, and ICT. The CSO Q4 2025 Quarterly National Accounts release confirms the 12.3% headline but does not publish a clean annual percentage-point decomposition by NACE sector [[3]].

| Sector | Role in 2025 GDP growth |
|---|---|
| Manufacturing (pharma & chemicals) | **Dominant driver** — MNE production, contract manufacturing, and pre-tariff export front-loading [[3]][[9]] |
| Information & communication (ICT) | Important positive contributor; also MNE-dominated [[3]] |
| Financial & insurance activities | Positive but smaller [[3]] |
| Construction | Domestic cyclical; not material to the 12.3% [[3]] |
| Distribution / retail / hospitality | Modest [[3]] |
| Public administration / education / health | Stable but not the source of the surge [[3]] |

**Critical interpretation:** Ireland publishes a *Modified Domestic Demand (MDD)* and *GNI** measure precisely because GDP is distorted by IP depreciation on foreign-owned intangibles, aircraft leasing, redomiciled corporate profits, MNE profit shifting, and contract manufacturing where goods are recorded as Irish output even when little physical production occurs in Ireland [[4]]. **The 2025 12.3% headline should not be read as a 12.3% rise in Irish living standards or domestic activity.** MDD and GNI* would show a far more modest expansion [[4]].

#### Malta (+4.0%), Cyprus (+3.8%), Croatia (+3.4%)

Small, highly open service economies. Approximate sector contributions (Eurostat A10 framework, with the caveat that chain-linked volumes are not perfectly additive) [[10]][[11]]:

| Country | Top sector contributors |
|---|---|
| Malta | Tourism / trade / transport / hospitality (~+0.8 to +1.0 pp); gaming / digital services (~+0.7 to +0.9 pp); professional, admin, ICT services (~+0.6 to +0.8 pp) [[10]][[11]][[12]] |
| Cyprus | Tourism / trade / transport / accommodation (~+0.8 to +1.0 pp); construction & real estate combined (~+0.5 to +0.7 pp); professional / ICT / financial services (~+0.6 to +0.8 pp) [[10]][[11]] |
| Croatia | Tourism / trade / transport / hospitality (~+0.8 to +1.0 pp); construction (~+0.4 to +0.6 pp, EU-funded); public, health, education and professional services (~+0.5 to +0.7 pp) [[10]][[11]] |

#### Poland (+3.6%) and Bulgaria (+3.1%) — domestic-demand-led convergence

| Country | Top sector contributors |
|---|---|
| Poland | Trade, transport, hospitality, consumer services (~+0.8 to +1.0 pp); manufacturing (~+0.5 to +0.7 pp); construction (~+0.4 to +0.5 pp, EU-fund-driven) [[10]][[11]] |
| Bulgaria | Trade, transport, hospitality (~+0.7 to +0.9 pp); ICT and professional/admin services (~+0.5 to +0.7 pp); construction and public services combined (~+0.4 to +0.6 pp) [[10]][[11]] |

#### Denmark (+2.9%) — a pharma anomaly

Denmark is the unique developed-economy outperformer, and it is **almost entirely pharma-driven**. Manufacturing — heavily Novo Nordisk and pharma exports — contributed roughly **+1.5 to +2.0 pp** of the 2.9% headline. Business services / ICT added ~+0.3 to +0.5 pp; public and health services ~+0.2 to +0.4 pp [[10]][[11]]. Domestic demand was steady but unspectacular.

#### Spain (+2.8%) — broad-based recovery

Trade/transport/hospitality/tourism (~+0.7 to +0.9 pp); professional, admin, and ICT services (~+0.5 to +0.7 pp); construction and public services combined (~+0.5 to +0.6 pp) [[10]][[11]].

### 5.2 Low-growth anomalies

#### Germany (+0.2%) — industrial recession deepening

Quantified Eurostat / national-accounts drags for 2025 [[2]]:

| Component | 2025 reading |
|---|---:|
| Manufacturing GVA | **−1.3%** |
| Construction GVA | **−3.6%** |
| Business services GVA | **−0.8%** |
| Gross fixed capital formation | **−0.5%** |
| ↳ Machinery & equipment investment | **−2.3%** |
| ↳ Construction investment | **−0.9%** |
| Exports | **−0.3%** |
| Imports | **+3.6%** |
| Net trade contribution to GDP | **≈ −1.5 pp** |

The losses concentrated in **autos, machinery/equipment, chemicals, and other energy-intensive industries** [[2]]. The European Commission attributes this to **higher US tariffs**, **euro appreciation**, **Chinese competition** in vehicles/machines/chemicals, and **front-loaded Q1 US exports that reversed in Q2** [[13]].

#### Finland (+0.2%) — demand-led weakness

Domestic demand excluding inventories declined in 2025; **private consumption fell sharply in H1** [[14]]. The earlier export-led recovery faded, and **investment dragged growth**. Residential investment had been cut back in prior years and was not expected to expand again until 2026 [[14]]. Net exports only added because weak domestic demand suppressed imports.

#### Italy (+0.5%)

Net foreign demand subtracted **−0.6 pp**, while domestic demand ex-inventories added +1.1 pp [[2]]. Industrial value added was −0.3% q/q and construction −0.2% q/q in Q3 [[2]]. The Commission estimated net exports subtracting −0.7 pp, with goods exports contracting 0.6%, and noted **falling housing investment as Superbonus tax credits were phased out** [[15]]. NRRP/RRF investment provided the only meaningful tailwind.

#### Hungary (+0.5%) — manufacturing-led contraction

KSH Q2 2025 sectoral data [[16]]:

| Sector | Q2 2025 YoY | Contribution to GDP |
|---|---:|---:|
| Industry | **−3.3%** | **−0.7 pp** |
| Manufacturing | **−4.4%** | (within industry) |
| Agriculture | **−11.4%** | **−0.3 pp** |
| Services | + | **+0.9 pp** |
| Construction | + | **+0.2 pp** |

Drivers: sluggish **machinery and vehicle exports**, weak industrial production, limited corporate investment [[15]].

#### Austria (+0.6%)

Industry-led weakness: investment, inventories, and industrial production all weak; **dwelling construction continued to decline**; net exports dampened growth [[15]]. **Exports to the US fell 14% in H1 2025**, mainly volatile chemicals [[15]]. High energy prices and rising unit labor costs reduced industrial competitiveness.

#### Estonia (+0.6%)

Statistics Estonia identifies **trade**, **transportation/storage**, and **construction** as the main drags [[17]]. Q4 quantified sector contributions [[17]]:

| Sector | Contribution (pp) |
|---|---:|
| Wholesale/retail trade | **−0.75** |
| Construction | **−0.40** |
| Health & social work | **−0.35** |
| Electricity/gas | −0.17 |
| Other services | −0.14 |
| Agriculture | −0.09 |
| Transport/storage | −0.07 |
| Real estate | −0.07 |

#### Luxembourg (+0.6%)

STATEC Q2 detail: GFCF fell, especially **non-residential construction** and **aircraft/satellites**; exports stagnated as goods and financial-service exports fell; **construction** and **transport** dragged value added [[18]]. Three years of quasi-stagnation and weak market-sector job creation [[18]].

#### France (+0.8%)

INSEE 2025 production data [[19]]:

| Component | 2025 |
|---|---:|
| Construction output | **−0.8%** |
| Household consumption | +0.4% |
| Gross fixed capital formation | +0.2% |
| ↳ GFCF in manufactured goods | **−2.6%** |
| ↳ Construction GFCF | −1.4% |
| Q4 manufacturing | −0.2% q/q |
| Q4 transport equipment | −0.2% q/q |
| Q4 automobiles | **−2.2%** q/q (factory shutdowns) |

The Commission cites **domestic political/economic uncertainty and fiscal adjustment** as the main drags; Q3-Q4 net-export support came largely from delivery of large transport equipment previously produced and stored [[15]].

#### Romania (+0.7%)

Growth held down by **fiscal consolidation** dampening private and public consumption, after a **9.3% of GDP deficit in 2024**. Current-account deficit remained large at **−7.9% of GDP** in 2025 [[15]].

#### Slovakia (+0.8%) — auto-tariff shock

Net exports contributed negatively; exports were expected to contract in H2 after Q1 front-loading ahead of US tariffs. Slovakia is highly exposed via its **large automotive industry** [[15]]. Domestically, private consumption decelerated after a VAT hike and amid uncertainty.

## 6. Macroeconomic Trends and Root Causes

### 6.1 The dominant 2025 macro themes

**(a) US tariff shock — the single most powerful cross-country differentiator.** Tariffs hit auto- and machinery-exposed economies hardest: Germany, Slovakia, Hungary, Austria, Italy. In all of them, exports were front-loaded in Q1 2025 and reversed sharply afterward [[13]][[15]]. Conversely, **Ireland benefited mechanically** in the headline numbers from pharma export front-loading ahead of feared US drug tariffs [[3]][[9]].

**(b) German industrial recession spillovers.** Germany's −1.3% manufacturing GVA and −3.6% construction GVA propagated through CEE supply chains, especially to Hungary (autos/machinery), Slovakia (autos), Czechia, and Austria [[2]][[13]].

**(c) ECB rate cuts and the lag of prior tightening.** Easing supported the recovery in services/real wages but the **lag of high rates** still depressed construction across Germany, Austria, Finland, Luxembourg, and France in 2025 [[14]][[15]][[18]][[19]].

**(d) Real wage recovery as inflation faded.** The dominant tailwind for the broad-based outperformers — Spain, Poland, Bulgaria, Croatia, Cyprus, Malta — was disinflation lifting real wages and consumption [[10]][[11]].

**(e) NextGenerationEU / RRF disbursements.** Material support to investment in Spain, Italy, Croatia, Bulgaria, Poland, and Romania [[10]][[11]][[15]]. Conversely, **Hungary's continued risk of EU-funds withholding** (rule-of-law conditionality) was a notable headwind cited by the IMF [[20]].

**(f) Tourism rebound.** Critical to Malta, Cyprus, Croatia, Spain — all small/medium open-services economies [[10]][[11]].

**(g) Fiscal consolidation.** A direct drag on Romania (post-9.3% deficit), France (political fiscal adjustment), Slovakia (VAT hike), and Estonia (tax hikes/VAT front-loading) [[15]].

## 7. Conclusion

The 2025 EU GDP picture is best summarized as **two-tier divergence with a single statistical outlier**:

1. **Ireland's 12.3% is not an economic boom** in the conventional sense — it is a multinational/IP/pharma accounting surge. Modified Domestic Demand and GNI* would tell a far more modest story [[3]][[4]].
2. **The genuine outperformers are small open service economies and CEE convergence economies** benefiting from tourism, real wage recovery, and EU funds — Malta, Cyprus, Croatia, Poland, Bulgaria, plus Spain [[10]][[11]].
3. **Denmark is the unique large developed-economy outperformer**, but its growth is concentrated in one company's pharma franchise [[10]][[11]] — so it shares with Ireland a vulnerability to drug-tariff or single-firm shocks.
4. **The underperformers are dominated by the EU's industrial core** — Germany, Italy, Austria, Hungary, Slovakia — all of them hit by some combination of US tariffs, China competition, and the legacy of energy-cost shocks [[2]][[13]][[15]]. Finland, Luxembourg, and Estonia represent a separate cluster of demand-side weakness amplified by geopolitical and fiscal-consolidation factors [[14]][[17]][[18]].

The most important macroeconomic story of EU GDP in 2025 is that the **trans-Atlantic tariff regime and German industrial recession** have replaced the energy-price shock as the binding drag on European industry, while **real-wage recovery and EU funds** are powering the smaller and southern/eastern economies in a way that meaningfully reshapes the EU's internal growth balance.
