# EU 2025 GDP: Anomalies, Industry Drivers, and Macroeconomic Context

**Date of report:** May 18, 2026
**Scope:** All 27 EU member states, calendar year 2025, with multi-year context and forward-looking forecasts

---

## 1. Executive Summary

The EU economy delivered **moderate, uneven growth in 2025**, with real GDP for the bloc up roughly **1.4%** (euro area 1.3%) [[1]]. The unweighted mean of the 27 member states' real GDP growth rates was **2.1%**; excluding Ireland's headline distortion (see below), the trimmed mean was **1.7%**.

Three patterns dominate:

1. **One extreme statistical outlier — Ireland at +12.3% [[2]]** — driven almost entirely by multinational enterprise (MNE) activity in pharmaceuticals, ICT, and contract manufacturing, with strong evidence of US-tariff-related export front-loading in early 2025. This is a measurement/MNE artefact, **not** a domestic boom: Ireland's modified GNI* indicator (the meaningful measure of domestic income) would have grown far less.

2. **A high-growth cluster of small, services-and-tourism-intensive economies plus Poland**: Malta (4.0%), Cyprus (3.8%), Poland (3.6%), Croatia (3.4%), and Bulgaria (3.1%) [[2]]. Common drivers: real-wage-supported consumption after disinflation, tourism strength, and EU Recovery and Resilience Facility (RRF) disbursements — for Croatia and Poland especially, EU-funded investment is central.

3. **A stagnation cluster of large continental industrial economies**: Germany (0.2%), Finland (0.2%), Italy (0.5%), Hungary (0.5%), Romania (0.7%) [[2]]. The common thread is **weak industry and weak investment**, but the country-specific bottlenecks differ — German manufacturing competitiveness loss; the Italian Superbonus unwind; Hungarian EU-funds blockage; Finnish housing/forestry exposure.

**The single most important analytical finding**: the EU's growth dispersion in 2025 is best explained by **economic structure**, not by cycle. Economies leaning on services exports, tourism, and EU transfers outperformed; economies leaning on energy-intensive manufacturing and goods exports underperformed. The ECB's rate-cutting cycle from 3.00% to 2.00% during H1 2025 [[1]] eased financing conditions but could not yet repair the structural problems in Germany's industrial core or unfreeze Hungary's EU-fund pipeline.

## 2. Methodology & Data Notes

- **Real GDP growth and 2024 comparison**: Eurostat `tec00115` ("Real GDP growth rate – volume"), latest 2025 vintage updated 2026-05-18 [[2]].
- **Nominal GDP 2025**: Eurostat `nama_10_gdp`, B1GQ at current market prices in million euros [[3]].
- **HICP inflation 2025**: Eurostat `prc_hicp_aind`, all-items annual average rate of change [[4]].
- **General government balance 2025**: Eurostat `gov_10dd_edpt1`, S13 net lending/borrowing, % of GDP [[5]].
- **2026–2027 forecasts**: European Commission Autumn 2025 Economic Forecast dataset [[6]].
- **Country-specific analysis**: Destatis [[7]], Bundesbank [[8]][[9]][[10]], IMF Article IV (Germany 2025) [[11]][[12]], European Commission country forecasts [[13]][[14]][[15]][[16]][[17]][[18]], CSO Ireland [[19]][[20]][[21]].
- **Macroeconomic context**: European Commission Autumn 2025 Forecast [[1]], ECB monetary policy statements [[22]][[23]][[24]][[25]][[26]], Eurostat EU–Russia trade [[27]], EU–US trade framework [[28]], NextGenerationEU progress [[29]].

**Caveats:**
- 2025 figures for several countries are Commission/Eurostat estimates and may be revised.
- **Ireland's GDP is not directly comparable**: CSO publishes a modified GNI* indicator precisely because headline GDP overstates domestic activity due to MNE profit-shifting, IP depreciation, and aircraft-leasing flows [[21]]. A specific 2025 real GNI* figure is not yet available, but qualitative evidence indicates underlying domestic growth was a fraction of headline GDP.
- Expenditure-side decompositions for the stagnation cluster (Finland, Italy, Hungary) are EC/AMECO-based directional estimates pending final national-accounts vintages.

## 3. Regional Overview — Macro Context for 2025

**Disinflation, easing, but no boom.** The European Commission's Autumn 2025 forecast projected EU GDP growth at **1.4%** (euro area 1.3%) and inflation falling to **2.5%** (euro area 2.1%) [[1]].

**Monetary policy — ECB cuts then pauses.** The ECB cut by 25 bp at four consecutive meetings (January, March, April, June 2025), taking the deposit rate from 3.00% to **2.00%** by June, then held through year-end [[22]][[23]][[24]][[25]][[26]]. This is a major change in financing conditions versus 2023–24 and supports rate-sensitive sectors (housing, capex) with a lag.

**Energy.** Energy inflation was negative in 2025, with European gas around **€32/MWh in Q4 2025** [[1]]. The acute energy shock has passed, but the post-2022 cost level remains structurally higher than pre-crisis and continues to weigh on energy-intensive industry (especially German chemicals and metals).

**Fiscal.** EU general government deficit at **3.3% of GDP** in 2025, slightly worse than 2024's 3.1%; overall fiscal stance broadly neutral [[1]]. By end-May 2025, RRF disbursements had reached **~€315 billion (49%)** of the envelope, with milestones due by 31 August 2026 and final payments by 31 December 2026 [[29]] — the funding tap is still flowing but with a clear cliff in sight.

**Trade shocks.** Two big external shocks defined 2025:
- The **EU–US trade framework** struck a headline US tariff of **15%** on many EU goods, with steel/aluminium higher [[28]]. This drove early-2025 export front-loading and then a persistent drag.
- **EU–Russia trade collapse**: between Q1 2022 and Q4 2025, EU exports to Russia fell **61%** and imports from Russia fell **90%** [[27]]. Russia is no longer a major direct trade partner; the geopolitical/sanctions/defence costs remain.

**Germany as systemic drag.** Germany's GDP in 2024 was roughly at its 2019 level, and 2025 growth was only **0.2%** [[14]]. As the EU's largest economy (~24% of EU nominal GDP — €4.47 trillion of €18.81 trillion [[3]]), Germany's stagnation mechanically holds down EU-wide aggregates.

## 6. Anomaly Analysis — High Growth

### 6.1 Ireland (+12.3%) — ★ Extreme outlier, MNE/measurement artefact

**Headline number must be qualified.** CSO Q4 2025 national accounts report real GDP up 12.3% in 2025 [[19]]. But Ireland's CSO publishes the **modified GNI\*** indicator specifically to strip out GDP distortions from redomiciled-company income, foreign IP depreciation, aircraft leasing, and other MNE profit/IP effects [[21]]. **A clean 2025 real GNI\* figure is not yet published, but it would unambiguously be far below 12.3%.** Ireland's GDP is not a like-for-like cross-country measure.

**Sector drivers (foreign MNE-dominated):**
- **Pharmaceuticals/chemicals and medical products** — within industry/manufacturing, by far the largest contributor
- **ICT/software and digital services** — major MNE contributor
- **Contract manufacturing** — production may physically occur abroad but is booked in Irish GDP when the Irish-resident principal owns the goods [[19]]

**Domestic sectors (construction, retail/hospitality, public services) grew modestly** — they cannot account for the 12.3% headline [[20]].

**One-off effect — US tariff front-loading.** CSO monthly trade releases for March and April 2025 show exceptional goods-export strength concentrated in pharma/chemical categories and US-linked flows, consistent with **front-loading ahead of the US tariff implementation** [[19]]. CSO does not publish a clean GDP-point estimate of this effect, but it is a meaningful share of the 2025 surge.

**Classification: STRUCTURAL (measurement) + CYCLICAL (tariff timing).** Ireland's headline GDP will always be MNE-distorted; on top of that, 2025 had a one-off tariff timing boost that the Commission expects to reverse — its forecast for Irish GDP in 2026 is just **+0.2%** [[6]], a startling implied payback consistent with the front-loading interpretation.

### 6.2 Malta (+4.0%), Cyprus (+3.8%), Poland (+3.6%), Croatia (+3.4%) — Genuine outperformers

**Malta** — services and tourism intensity
- **Sector drivers**: ICT/digital services (including gaming), financial services, professional/administrative services, tourism-related accommodation/food/transport [[16]]. Manufacturing is small.
- **Expenditure side**: Private consumption strongest (driven by employment growth, population inflows, wage growth); investment positive; services exports support net exports though import leakage is heavy [[16]].
- **Macro context**: Tourism above pre-pandemic levels, high-value services FDI, digital/financial-services expansion.

**Cyprus** — tourism + property + ICT/professional services
- **Sector drivers**: Tourism/accommodation, construction/real estate, ICT and professional services (boosted by company relocations and headquartering), financial services [[15]].
- **Expenditure side**: Private consumption and investment lead; tourism exports help, partly offset by import-heavy domestic demand [[15]].
- **Macro context**: Tourism rebound, property/construction cycle, RRF-funded green/digital projects. Notably running a **government surplus of +3.4%** [[5]] and an HICP of just 0.8% [[4]] — best fiscal/inflation combination in the EU.

**Croatia** — the classic "tourism + EU funds" case
- **Sector drivers**: Construction (the clearest engine, driven by EU-funded infrastructure, reconstruction, public investment), tourism-related accommodation/food/transport/retail, public services, professional/ICT services [[17]]. Manufacturing is weaker.
- **Expenditure side**: Private consumption is largest contributor (helped by real wages, employment, tourism income); investment very strong on RRF/cohesion funds; net exports likely a drag because consumption and investment pull in imports faster than tourism receipts grow [[17]].
- **Macro context**: Croatia is a textbook EU-funds case — but HICP at **4.4%** [[4]] (the joint-highest in the EU with Hungary) signals overheating risk.

**Poland** — consumption- and EU-fund-led recovery
- **Sector drivers**: Market services, construction (improving as public/EU-funded investment resumes), manufacturing recovery, ICT/business services [[18]]. Tourism plays a comparatively minor macro role.
- **Expenditure side**: Private consumption is the largest driver — **rapid real wage growth, disinflation, tight labour market**; investment rebounds with RRF disbursements after the post-2023 unblocking; net exports a drag because stronger demand pulls in imports [[18]].
- **Macro context**: The single most important fact about Poland 2025 is that **the EU-funds tap reopened** after the rule-of-law dispute resolution in 2024. But this came with a **−7.3% of GDP fiscal deficit** [[5]] — the second-worst in the EU after Romania — driven by defence spending and election-related fiscal support.

**Classification: All four are STRUCTURAL outperformers** — small open services-led economies (Malta, Cyprus) and post-cohesion convergence economies (Poland, Croatia) with EU funds amplifying domestic-demand strength. Continued above-EU growth is forecast for 2026 (Malta 3.8%, Poland 3.5%, Croatia 2.9%, Cyprus 2.6% [[6]]).

## 7. Anomaly Analysis — Low Growth / Stagnation

### 7.1 Germany (+0.2%) — STRUCTURAL, the single most consequential anomaly

**The decisive economy.** As 24% of EU nominal GDP [[3]], Germany's 0.2% growth is the most important fact in the EU 2025 picture. Destatis confirms 0.2% real GDP growth, with the recession concentrated in industry and construction [[7]].

**Sector breakdown (real GVA, 2025) [[7]]:**

| Sector | 2025 GVA | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| Manufacturing | **−1.3%** | Losses in motor vehicles, machinery/equipment, chemicals |
| Construction | **−3.6%** | Deep recession; building hit by financing costs; civil engineering relatively better |
| Business services | −0.8% | Reflects weak industrial/investment demand |
| Other services | −0.3% | Slightly negative |
| Trade, transport, hotels | +1.2% | Retail solid, accommodation weaker |
| Information & communication | +1.3% | Continuing expansion |
| Public services, education, health | +1.4% | Stabiliser — strongest positive sector |

**Expenditure side (real, 2025) [[7]]:**

| Component | Change | Comment |
|---|---:|---|
| Household consumption | +1.4% | Real wage recovery + low unemployment |
| Government consumption | +1.5% | Public demand support |
| Gross fixed capital formation | −0.5% | Investment still contracting |
| — Construction investment | −0.9% | Housing weakness persists |
| — Machinery & equipment | −2.3% | Business capex depressed |
| Exports | −0.3% | Goods exports fell — vehicles, machinery, chemicals |
| Imports | +3.6% | Imports up as domestic demand recovers |
| **Net exports** | **≈ −1.5 pp** | **Largest single drag on GDP** |

**Macro drivers (structural):**
1. **Energy competitiveness** — pre-2022 model relied on cheap pipeline gas; post-shock cost level remains structurally elevated; hits chemicals, metals, basic manufacturing [[11]][[10]].
2. **Auto sector / EV transition** — German strength in premium ICE vehicles; weaker EV positioning, especially in China where Chinese EV makers now dominate; Bundesbank flags persistent decline in German motor-vehicle output and lost export market share [[8]][[9]][[10]].
3. **Chinese competition** — China has moved from buyer of German capital goods to direct competitor in EVs, machinery, electrical equipment, chemicals [[10]].
4. **US tariffs** — 15% US tariffs on EU goods, plus higher on steel/aluminium [[28]]; major hit to German export model [[7]][[11]].
5. **Demographic constraints** — IMF warns Germany's working-age population set to decline more sharply than other advanced economies [[11]][[12]]; structural ceiling on potential growth.
6. **Fiscal pivot just starting** — In 2025, Germany exempted defence above 1% of GDP from fiscal rules, created a **€500 billion infrastructure/climate fund**, and eased Länder borrowing [[13]]. This is a major regime change but largely back-loaded to 2026–27.

**Classification: STRUCTURAL.** Energy costs, EV transition, Chinese competition, and demographics are not cyclical. The EC forecasts Germany at 1.2% in both 2026 and 2027 [[6]] — a recovery, but a slow one; the new infrastructure/defence fund should be the swing factor.

### 7.2 Finland (+0.2%) — STRUCTURAL (Russia, forestry) + CYCLICAL (rate-sensitive housing)

**Sector drivers** [[14]]:
- **Construction/housing** — main domestic drag; Finland's housing cycle is highly rate-sensitive (variable-rate mortgages reset quickly), so even with ECB cuts the rebound is slow.
- **Forestry, wood products, pulp and paper** — weak/stagnant; European construction weakness hurts sawn wood; paper faces structural decline plus cyclical demand.
- **Electronics/electrical equipment** — weak global capex/telecom demand.
- **Russia-linked trade, transit, border services** — structurally impaired after 2022 collapse [[27]].

**Expenditure decomposition (approximate, EC/AMECO) [[14]]:**
- Private consumption: +0.2 pp (cautious households)
- Government consumption: −0.1 to −0.2 pp (fiscal consolidation)
- Investment: **−0.5 to −0.8 pp** (residential and corporate capex weak)
- Net exports: +0.4 to +0.7 pp (positive mainly because imports weaker than exports — not a healthy export boom)

**Classification: Mostly STRUCTURAL** (lost Russia, forestry headwinds) + **CYCLICAL** (rate-sensitive housing should recover with ECB cuts). EC forecasts 0.9% in 2026, 1.2% in 2027 [[6]].

### 7.3 Italy (+0.5%) — STRUCTURAL (manufacturing) + CYCLICAL (Superbonus unwind)

**Sector drivers** [[14]]:
- **Manufacturing/industry stagnant or contracting** — weakness concentrated in energy-intensive manufacturing, machinery, intermediate goods, textiles/fashion, export-facing industries.
- **Construction losing previous impulse** — the **post-Superbonus unwind** (the lavish home-renovation tax credit that drove Italian growth in 2022–23) means residential renovation no longer adds to GDP.
- **Services** — main stabiliser via tourism, personal services, parts of business services.

**Expenditure decomposition (approximate) [[14]]:**
- Private consumption: +0.3 to +0.5 pp (employment, recovering real wages)
- Government consumption: ≈ 0 to +0.1 pp (fiscal consolidation limits)
- Investment: **−0.2 to −0.4 pp** (Superbonus correction; NRRP partly offsets)
- Net exports: roughly neutral / slightly negative

**Classification: Mostly CYCLICAL** (Superbonus is a one-off; manufacturing weakness partly imported from Germany) but with **STRUCTURAL** elements (chronic productivity gap, high public debt limits fiscal space, government balance at −3.1% [[5]] just at EU rule limit). EC forecasts 0.8% in 2026 and 2027 [[6]] — sustained low growth.

### 7.4 Hungary (+0.5%) — STRUCTURAL (EU-funds freeze, auto exposure)

**Sector drivers** [[14]]:
- **Industry, especially automotive and related supply chains** — Hungary is one of the most car-export-intensive economies in the EU, tied to German/European production and the EV-transition turbulence.
- **Investment-linked sectors contracting** — construction, public infrastructure, corporate capex hit by high financing costs **and delayed EU-funded projects**.
- **Consumption-facing services** recover from a low base after the inflation shock.

**Expenditure decomposition (approximate) [[14]]:**
- Private consumption: **+1.5 to +2.0 pp** (real wages recover after 2022–23 inflation shock — HICP still 4.4% in 2025, tied highest in EU [[4]])
- Government consumption: +0.2 to +0.4 pp (pre-election fiscal support; 2026 is an election year)
- Investment: **−1.0 to −1.5 pp** — **the decisive drag, driven by EU-funds freeze from rule-of-law disputes**
- Net exports: −0.5 to −1.0 pp (auto export weakness + import leakage from consumption)

Government balance at **−4.7% of GDP** [[5]] reflects the pre-election fiscal stance.

**Classification: STRUCTURAL.** EU-funds blockages are a policy issue, not a cycle, and auto-sector exposure is a long-term structural choice. EC forecasts 2.3% in 2026 and 2.1% in 2027 [[6]] — a meaningful acceleration that would require funds-resolution and auto stabilisation.

### 7.5 Other low-growth flags (less severe)

**Austria (+0.6%)**, **Estonia (+0.6%)**, **Luxembourg (+0.6%)** — All grew modestly but only after 2024 contractions; not yet at pre-shock levels. Austria's weakness is industrial/German-supply-chain-linked; Estonia's is Russia/Nordic-trade and rate-sensitive consumer; Luxembourg's reflects its outsized financial-services exposure to euro-area conditions.

**Romania (+0.7%)** — Slowest growth among the central/eastern convergence countries, paired with a **−7.9% fiscal deficit** [[5]] (worst in EU) and **6.8% HICP** [[4]] (highest in EU). Romania faces the cost of fiscal consolidation in 2026.

## 8. Industry Decomposition — Where the Shifts Came From

Pulling the country-level evidence together, three industries explain most of the dispersion in 2025:

| Industry | Where it drove growth | Where it dragged | Net direction in EU |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pharmaceuticals/chemicals (high-value)** | Ireland (decisive) | Germany (energy-intensive chemicals) | Pharma exports dominated by Ireland's MNE pipeline; basic chemicals in retreat |
| **Automotive & machinery** | — | Germany, Hungary, Slovakia (auto-supply-chain) | Significant drag; EV transition + Chinese competition + US tariffs |
| **Construction** | Croatia, Poland (EU-funded infra); Cyprus (real estate) | Germany (−3.6% GVA), Finland (housing), Italy (Superbonus unwind) | Bifurcated — EU-funded infrastructure up, market housing down |
| **ICT/digital services** | Malta, Cyprus, Ireland, Poland | — | Broadly positive, urban-services-led growth |
| **Tourism & accommodation** | Croatia, Cyprus, Malta, Spain, Greece, Portugal | — | Strong contributor across the Mediterranean |
| **Financial & professional services** | Malta, Cyprus, Luxembourg, Ireland | — | Positive but mature |
| **Forestry/pulp/paper** | — | Finland | Structural decline + cyclical demand weakness |
| **Public services** | Germany (stabiliser), most countries | — | Universal positive contributor |

The big asymmetry: **services and tourism economies (Mediterranean + Malta) outperformed; goods-manufacturing economies (Germany, Hungary, Slovakia, Finland) underperformed.** This reflects the post-2022 structural shift in European competitiveness — the energy-cost shock and Chinese competition hit manufacturing far harder than services.

## 9. Expenditure Decomposition — Demand-Side Cross-Country Patterns

| Country | Consumption | Investment | Govt | Net exports | Headline GDP |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Germany | +1.4% (positive) | −0.5% (drag) | +1.5% (positive) | **≈ −1.5 pp drag** | 0.2% [[7]] |
| Poland | Largest driver (+) | Rebounding (+) | + | Drag | 3.6% [[18]] |
| Croatia | Largest driver (+) | Very strong (+) | + | Drag | 3.4% [[17]] |

**Key patterns:**
- **Net exports are a drag in most of the growth leaders.** Poland, Croatia, and Germany all face negative net exports — for the growth leaders it's because strong domestic demand pulls in imports; for Germany it's because exports themselves are weak.
- **Investment is the swing variable.** It is the decisive engine in Croatia and Poland (EU funds) and the decisive drag in Germany, Finland, and Hungary.
- **Consumption is universally positive but not uniformly strong.** Real wage recovery after disinflation lifted consumption everywhere, but it cannot offset weak investment.

| Poland | +3.6% | STRUCTURAL (convergence + EU funds) | Continued strong — EC 2026: 3.5% [[6]] |
| Croatia | +3.4% | STRUCTURAL (tourism + EU funds) | Continued strong — EC 2026: 2.9% [[6]] |
| Germany | +0.2% | STRUCTURAL (energy, autos, demographics) | Gradual improvement — EC 2026: 1.2% [[6]] |
| Finland | +0.2% | STRUCTURAL (Russia, forestry) + CYCLICAL (housing) | Slow rebound — EC 2026: 0.9% [[6]] |
| Italy | +0.5% | CYCLICAL (Superbonus unwind) + STRUCTURAL (productivity) | Persistent low growth — EC 2026: 0.8% [[6]] |
| Hungary | +0.5% | STRUCTURAL (EU funds, autos) | Acceleration if funds unblock — EC 2026: 2.3% [[6]] |

**Read across:** the high-growth anomalies are **persistent**; the low-growth anomalies are **mostly persistent** with Hungary and Finland the most likely to improve (one on EU-funds resolution, the other on the rate cycle).

## 11. Macroeconomic Themes & Root Causes

Five themes cut across the country analysis:

**1. The post-2022 energy shock has become structural in heavy industry.** Energy costs are no longer rising, but they have settled at a higher level than pre-2022. Germany's chemicals, metals, and energy-intensive manufacturing have lost competitiveness that they have not recovered [[11]][[10]]. This explains the Germany-Italy-Slovakia stagnation cluster.

**2. The China shock has flipped.** China is no longer just a buyer of German capital goods — it is a direct competitor in EVs, machinery, electrical equipment, and chemicals. German auto export market share has declined persistently [[10]]. Hungary's auto-dependent model is exposed via the same channel.

**3. The US tariff shock added a new layer of trade friction.** The 15% US tariff baseline on EU goods (with steel/aluminium higher) [[28]] created a one-off front-loading boost in Q1 2025 (especially Irish pharma) followed by a persistent drag on net exports. Germany the most exposed; Ireland's 12.3% headline is partly a transient artefact of this.

**4. EU funds are the great differentiator.** RRF disbursements at €315bn / 49% by May 2025 [[29]] have driven Croatia and Poland's outperformance and Spain/Portugal's solid growth. Hungary's growth is depressed substantially because of the EU-funds freeze. The August 2026 milestone deadline and December 2026 final payment deadline create a meaningful **fiscal cliff** for net-recipient economies in 2027.

**5. The ECB rate cycle is now supportive — but with country-asymmetric effects.** The cut from 3.00% to 2.00% [[22]–[26]] helps rate-sensitive housing markets (Finland, Sweden, Netherlands) and reduces sovereign debt service. But it doesn't fix structural problems in industrial Germany, Italy, or auto-dependent Hungary.

---

## 12. Policy Context

**Monetary policy.** ECB deposit rate at 2.00% by mid-2025 [[26]], held through year-end [[26]]. From a deeply restrictive stance to mildly accommodative — a major regime change supporting investment and housing with a lag. Non-euro members: Sweden, Denmark in line; Hungary, Poland, Czechia easing more slowly given residual inflation (HICP 4.4%, 3.3%, 2.3% respectively [[4]]).

**Fiscal stance — country dispersion is large.**
- **Surpluses (>0):** Cyprus +3.4%, Denmark +2.9%, Ireland +1.8%, Greece +1.7%, Portugal +0.7% [[5]] — peripheral/small economies with strong fiscal discipline.
- **Around EU rule (−3% limit):** Italy −3.1%, Croatia −3.0% [[5]] — at the limit.
- **Excessive deficits:** Romania −7.9%, Poland −7.3%, Belgium −5.2%, France −5.1%, Hungary −4.7% [[5]] — five member states well above 3%, signalling consolidation pressure ahead.

**EU policy.** RRF disbursements at €315bn / 49% by May 2025, with 31 August 2026 milestone deadline and 31 December 2026 final payment deadline [[29]] — net-recipient economies face a substantial step-down in 2027. Germany's €500bn infrastructure fund [[13]] is the largest new fiscal-policy initiative and largely back-loaded.

**Trade policy.** The 15% US tariff framework [[28]] is now a permanent feature of the 2025–26 macro environment, with steel/aluminium tariffs higher and pharma carve-outs partial.

## 13. Risks & Forward-Looking Assessment

**For 2026 (EC Autumn 2025 Forecast [[6]]):**
- **EU growth is forecast to pick up to ~1.5%** as ECB cuts feed through, Germany's fiscal pivot kicks in, and Hungarian/CEE investment recovers.
- **Ireland forecast at +0.2%** — the starkest single-country swing, embedding the reversal of tariff front-loading.
- **Germany at +1.2%** — a recovery but not a boom; depends on infrastructure fund execution.
- **The high-growth cluster persists**: Poland 3.5%, Malta 3.8%, Croatia 2.9%, Cyprus 2.6%.

**Key risks:**
1. **NextGenerationEU cliff** in 2027 as RRF expires — Croatia, Poland, Spain, Italy, and other large recipients face a meaningful fiscal/investment step-down [[29]].
2. **Further US tariff escalation** — the 15% framework could be renegotiated or expanded; pharma carve-outs are not guaranteed.
3. **German fiscal pivot execution risk** — the €500bn fund is large but will only matter if disbursement is fast and well-targeted.
4. **Hungary EU-funds resolution risk** — if rule-of-law disputes are not resolved, the 2.3% 2026 forecast [[6]] will undershoot.
5. **China demand and competition** — further weakening of Chinese demand for German capital goods, or further EV/machinery competitive pressure, would deepen the industrial stagnation cluster.

**Structural takeaway.** The 2025 dispersion is not noise — it is the EU's economic geography reorganising around three forces: the post-2022 energy cost level, the China–US trade restructuring, and EU transfer flows. The economies aligned with services, tourism, and EU funds are winning; the economies built on energy-intensive heavy manufacturing and goods exports to China and the US are losing. This pattern is likely to persist into 2026–27.
